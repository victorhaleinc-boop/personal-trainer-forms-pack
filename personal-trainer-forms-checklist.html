The core workflow

Six trainer forms, in the order most businesses actually need them

  1. Client intake form: collect health history, background, goals, stress, and scheduling constraints before you design anything.
  2. PAR-Q screening: identify clients who should get medical clearance before entering a training program.
  3. Informed consent and waiver: document exercise risk acknowledgement and protect the business from vague verbal agreements.
  4. Fitness assessment: capture baseline strength, movement quality, posture, and cardiovascular starting points.
  5. Package agreement: lock in payment terms, cancellation policy, session expiry, and client responsibilities.
  6. Session notes: track exercises, sets, modifications, client feedback, and no-show patterns over time.

1. Intake form

Your intake form should answer one question fast: is this person straightforward to coach, or is there hidden risk and context you need before session one?

2. PAR-Q

A proper PAR-Q catches red flags like dizziness, joint issues, blood pressure concerns, pregnancy, diabetes, or other conditions that change how you train. Use the free PAR-Q sample if you want a starting point.

3. Waiver

A waiver is not magic legal armor, but it is still basic operational hygiene. It sets expectations clearly and proves the client acknowledged exercise risk. Use the free sample waiver if you want a starting point.

4. Fitness assessment

Without a baseline, clients often feel like they are "working hard" without seeing evidence. An assessment gives you comparison points later.

5. Package agreement

This is where most new trainers get soft. If session expiry, no-show rules, and payment timing are not written down, you will negotiate them repeatedly.

6. Session notes

Session notes stop you from re-learning the same client every week. They also make progress reviews far easier and more credible.

Practical Checklist

What new trainers usually miss

  • They collect goals but not contraindications.
  • They use a waiver but never ask about medications or previous injuries.
  • They sell packages verbally, then argue about late cancels and session expiry later.
  • They run sessions from memory, which makes progression less consistent and progress reviews weaker.
  • They keep forms in too many places: texts, notes app, DMs, and random PDFs.

A small paperwork system beats a sophisticated mess. The goal is not bureaucracy. The goal is fewer avoidable mistakes while you coach real people.

FAQ

Questions trainers ask before buying paperwork templates

Can I use these forms with online clients?
Yes. Online trainers still need screening, consent, goals, baseline notes, and package terms. The wording may need slight edits based on delivery style.
Do I still need a lawyer to review the waiver and agreement?
Yes. Templates are a practical starting point, not legal advice. Local review matters if you want language that matches your jurisdiction and business setup.
Can I turn these into fillable forms?
Yes. Most buyers paste them into Google Docs, Jotform, Typeform, Practice Better, or another client intake system and customize from there.
Is this overkill for a solo trainer with only a few clients?
No. That is the stage where paperwork gaps hurt most because every client conversation is still manual and easy to lose track of.
